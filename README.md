# 📊 Project Cycle 3: Two-Sample Proportion Inference on YRBS 2007

## 📖 Project Overview
This project analyzes the 2007 Youth Risk Behavior Survey (YRBS 2007) dataset to investigate a critical public health and behavioral question: **"Is the proportion of high school students who felt sad or hopeless significantly different between female and male students?"**

By developing a structured and reproducible data science pipeline, we perform comprehensive data cleaning, variable recoding, and a formal Two-Proportion Z-Test to derive statistically sound conclusions.

## 🎯 Statistical Framework & Hypotheses
* **Methodology**: Two-Sample Proportion Hypothesis Testing (Two-sided Z-Test)
* **Significance Level ($\alpha$)**: 0.05
* **Null Hypothesis ($H_0$)**: $p_{female} - p_{male} = 0$ (There is no difference in the proportion of feeling sad or hopeless between female and male students)
* **Alternative Hypothesis ($H_A$)**: $p_{female} - p_{male} \neq 0$ (There is a significant difference in the proportion of feeling sad or hopeless between female and male students)

## 📊 Key Findings
* **Sample Size**: A final usable sample of **13,833** students (Female: 6,940; Male: 6,893) after a minimal 1.48% data attrition rate.
* **Descriptive Stats**: Female students exhibited a sadness/hopelessness proportion of **37.18%**, whereas male students exhibited **22.73%**. The estimated empirical difference is **14.44%**.
* **Inferential Stats**: 
  * **Z-Statistic**: 18.5374
  * **P-value**: $< 0.0001$ (approx. $1.03 \times 10^{-76}$)
  * **95% Confidence Interval**: `[12.94%, 15.95%]` (Does not contain 0)
* **Conclusion**: Since the P-value is overwhelmingly smaller than $\alpha = 0.05$, we **reject the null hypothesis ($H_0$)**. There is extraordinarily strong evidence to conclude that female high school students have a significantly higher proportion of feeling sad or hopeless compared to male students.

*(Note: As this study relies purely on observational survey data, these results demonstrate a robust statistical association but cannot be used to infer direct causality.)*
